Travelling with medication

Make sure your medication won't cause you any problems when you go through airport and border security screenings, while you're abroad or when you return.

Check that your medication is legal

Your prescription medication or other drugs, supplements or herbal and homeopathic products that are legal in Canada may be illegal in other countries.

Before you go:

  • check destination advice and advisories for local laws
  • contact the embassy or consulate of the country you plan to visit to make sure that your medication or medical supplies are allowed there

Cannabis

Cannabis is legal in Canada. However, it is an illegal drug in most countries. It is illegal to take cannabis or any product containing cannabis across Canada’s international borders. This applies even to places that have legalized or decriminalized it.

You can be arrested for using or transporting cannabis, even for medical purposes.

Cannabis and international travel provides more information.

Tips for travelling with your medication

When travelling abroad with prescription or over-the-counter medication:

  • pack all medication in its original, labelled container
  • don’t try to save luggage space by combining medication in a single container
  • pack all medications in your carry-on baggage to make airport security and customs screening easier
    • the limit of two carry-on bags does not apply to medical supplies, equipment and mobility aids
    • prescription medication is exempt from the liquid restrictions but you must present it to the screening officer separately from your carry-on baggage
    • consult What you can bring on a plane to determine what you can and cannot pack in your carry-on luggage
  • use the Family/Special Needs security line if you need to as screening officers there are trained to offer additional assistance
  • pack an extra supply of medication in case you are away for longer than expected
  • carry a copy of your original prescription and ensure that it includes both the generic and trade names of your medication in case of loss or theft
  • bring a doctor's note describing why you are taking the medication if possible

Do not buy medication outside Canada unless you have been advised to do so by a health care professional. Be wary of counterfeit medications or those that may not meet Canadian standards.

Travelling to the United States

If you are a Canadian visiting the United States, your personal medication may be subject to U.S. drug importation laws and regulations. If your medication is not available in the U.S. you can generally bring a 90-day supply with you.

U.S. Food and Drug Administration - Personal Importation provides more information.

Bringing medication back to Canada

Health Canada generally permits you to return from abroad with a limited amount of prescription medication. The same rules apply if you are bringing over-the-counter medication back with you.

Based on directions for use, you may bring whichever is less:

  • a 90-day supply
  • a single course of treatment

The drug must be shipped/carried in one of the following:

  • hospital or pharmacy dispensed packaging
  • original retail packaging
  • a container with the original label affixed, clearly indicating what the health product is and what it contains

Import requirements for health products provides more information.
